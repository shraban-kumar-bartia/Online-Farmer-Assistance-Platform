O R A N G E (‡§∏‡§Ç‡§§‡§∞‡§æ)

Botanical Name:Citrus √ó sinensis

Sun Exposure:Full sun (6-8 hours of direct sunlight daily)

Plant Type:Evergreen tree

Soil pH:5.5 to 7.5 (slightly acidic to slightly alkaline)

Bloom Time:5.5 to 7.5 (slightly acidic to slightly alkaline)

Flower Color:White with a fragrant aroma

Hardiness Zones:9-11 (USDA Plant Hardiness Zones)

Where We Grow

Site Selection

  • Climate: Oranges thrive in subtropical climates with warm summers and mild winters. Frost-prone areas are unsuitable.
  • Soil: Well-drained, sandy loam soil with a slightly acidic pH (5.0-6.5) is optimal. Avoid heavy clay soils.
  • Sunlight: Choose a site that receives full sun for the best growth and sweetest fruit.

Land Preparation

  • Clear and Till: Remove any existing vegetation and thoroughly till the soil to improve drainage and aeration.
  • Soil Amendments: If necessary, amend the soil with compost or manure to improve its fertility and organic matter content.

Climate

  • Temperature: Oranges prefer a temperature range of 25-35¬∞C (77-95¬∞F) during the growing season. Temperatures below 13¬∞C (55¬∞F) can harm young trees, and frost is detrimental.
  • Rainfall: Oranges require adequate rainfall, around 100-150 cm annually, well-distributed throughout the year. In arid regions, irrigation is essential.

How We Grow

steps to grow

  • Variety Selection: Choose orange varieties suitable for your climate and your intended market (fresh consumption, juicing, etc.). Consider disease resistance too.
  • Planting: Plant young, grafted trees in the prepared ground. The ideal planting time is spring or early fall.
  • Spacing: Provide ample space between trees for optimal growth. Spacing requirements depend on the rootstock and variety of oranges.

Care And Maintenance

Irrigation

  • Water regularly and deeply, especially during dry periods, flowering, and fruit development. Avoid overwatering, which can lead to root rot.
  • Orange trees are moderately drought-tolerant but require consistent moisture, especially during the first few years after planting and during hot, dry weather.
  • Aim to keep the soil consistently moist, but not soggy. Overwatering can lead to root rot.

Pruning

  • Prune young trees for shaping and proper branching. Mature trees require light annual pruning for airflow, sunlight penetration, and to remove dead or diseased branches.
  • Pruning is essential for maintaining the health and shape of your orange tree, and for encouraging fruit production.
  • Prune your tree regularly, preferably during the dormant season (late winter or early spring).

Pest and Disease Control

  • Monitor regularly for common orange pests and diseases like citrus greening, scale insects, and fungal diseases. Implement integrated pest management (IPM) strategies.
  • Orange trees are susceptible to a number of pests and diseases, including aphids, mites, scale, and fungal diseases.
  • It is important to monitor your trees for signs of pests and diseases and take action to control them promptly.

Weeding

  • Keep the orchard floor weed-free, especially around the base of the trees, to minimize competition for resources.
  • Weeds compete with your orange tree for water and nutrients.
  • It is important to control weeds around your tree to ensure that it gets the resources it needs to thrive.

Fertilizer

  • Apply a balanced fertilizer according to the age of the trees and soil test results. Split the application across the growing season.
  • Orange trees need to be fertilized regularly to ensure that they have the nutrients they need to grow and produce fruit.
  • Use a fertilizer that is specifically formulated for citrus trees.

Harvesting

  • Oranges are typically harvested when they reach full size and develop their characteristic color.
  • Oranges are ready to harvest when they have reached their mature size and color, which varies depending on the variety.
  • The peel of a ripe orange should be firm but slightly yielding to gentle pressure.

Storage and Control

  • Oranges can be stored for short periods at room temperature. For extended storage, a cool environment (around 4-8¬∞C) and high humidity are necessary.
  • Ripe oranges can be stored at room temperature for up to a week.
  • For longer storage, oranges can be stored in the refrigerator for up to 2 weeks.
